2.2 Digital Twin Applications in Elderly Care
Elderly care has become a major focus point for digital twin technology. This technology creates virtual versions of elderly residents that accurately reflect their current physical and physiological condition. Also, unlike the previous static monitoring systems, the digital twin technology for elderly care uses multi-source sensor data, behavioral pattern data, and environmental data to create a complete digital representation of an individual elderly resident that is updated in real-time to match the current health status for that individual elderly resident. In a residential care facility, this technology allows care workers to identify potential health risks (e.g. cardiovascular anomalies or falls) at a much earlier stage as they analyze longer-term health trends thereby allowing for more efficient use of available resources. In studies that have recently been completed, the findings confirmed that the care of elderly individuals using the digital twin-based care systems resulted in reduced emergency hospital admissions and more efficient use of resources on a daily basis for the elderly. The technical basis of these systems relies upon the use of real-time data ingestion protocols, intellectual collaborative processing at the edge and in the cloud, and intellectual anomaly detection; these will be critical to 
bridging
 the gap between data collection and actionable care decisions for the elderly living in long-term care homes (Liu, et al., 2019).
2.2.1 Personalized Health Baseline Establishment via Multi-Source Data Fusion
Digital twin technology can be utilised to create tailored health baselines for each individual residing within the elderly care sector. As a foundation to health analysis and anomaly detection, digital twins utilise and integrate multiple sources of information, such as real-time physiological information (i.e. data from wearable devices) and behavioural/activity data from ambient sensing (i.e. indoor movement or activity duration) in order to generate a digital profile that is unique to each resident. This digital profile is continuously updated as new data is captured through ongoing collecting, which allows adjustment due to gradual changes in physiology and activity level. For example, an elderly person may experience decreasing mobility because of their age, which would result in decreased heart rate baselines for that particular elderly person's chronic 
disease management. In this way, when conducting health analysis using this designed baseline of an elderly resident, there will not be general population thresholds used and therefore, there is an accurate measurement from which to assess deviation (from what is considered normal) regarding health and behavioural 
state;
 allowing for the basis of a more contextually relevant assessment.

 Indoor Positioning Technologies for Elderly Monitoring
Accurate indoor localization is a foundational requirement for in-home elderly monitoring systems, as the position of an individual within their residence provides essential context for detecting abnormal behaviors such as prolonged immobility, falls, or deviation from daily routines. Unlike outdoor environments where Global Positioning System (GPS) provides reliable tracking, indoor settings present significant challenges due to signal attenuation, multipath propagation, and non-line-of-sight conditions caused by walls, furniture, and other structural elements (
Alarifi
 et al., 2016). Consequently, a range of alternative indoor positioning technologies have been explored in the research literature, each offering different trade-offs among accuracy, cost, power consumption, and privacy preservation.

.2 BLE RSSI-Based Indoor Positioning
BLE-based indoor positioning systems most often rely on two types of algorithms: distance-based trilateration and fingerprinting-based classification. With trilateration, the Received Signal Strength Indicator (RSSI) of the BLE signals transmitted by the anchor nodes are used to estimate the distances to the target device. By using a 
long
-distance path loss model, the RSSI measurements can be transformed into an estimation of the distance between the device and each anchor node (Faragher & Harle, 2015). The 
long
-distance path loss model follows the equation RSSI = A 鈭?10n路log鈧佲個(d), where A is the RSSI measured at one meter, n is the path loss exponent which is specific to the surrounding space and d is the distance between the transmitter and receiver. However, due to the multipath fading effects caused by obstacles such as furniture, human bodies and echoes, the noise in the RSSI signal causes a large deviation in the distance estimation.
Signal Smoothing Using Kalman Filter Algorithm 
RSSI
 often have tendency to fluctuate. Several 
research
 employ Kalman filter as a method to smooth those signals. Authors in Gomez-de-Gabriel et al. 
(2018
) propose 
a
 approach that utilizing the channel diversity method along with Kalman filter and weighted trilateration to achieve an accuracy of 1.82 meters for mobile objects in medium size indoor area and 0.7 meters for static objects. Another paper by 
Dwiputranto
 et al. 
(2023
) mentions that a method to apply a Kalman filter to the raw BLE RSSI data is capable of improve the location accuracy from 0.90 m to 0.78 m and precision from 0.36 m to 0.23 m when applied with min-max trilateration.
While BLE RSSI trilateration alone provides zone-level accuracy sufficient for identifying which room an elderly individual occupies, finer-grained status 
determinations 
such as distinguishing whether a person is lying on a bed versus lying on the floor 
beside 
requires additional sensing modalities. Perez-Navarro et al. (2020) compared Wi-Fi and BLE based indoor positioning systems in real residential scenarios for monitoring older adults and found that BLE-based approaches, when combined with environmental context, provided more reliable room-level detection than Wi-Fi fingerprinting alone. Gomez-de-Gabriel et al. (2018) demonstrated that integrating channel diversity with Kalman filtering and weighted trilateration achieved positioning accuracy of 0.7 meters for static targets and 1.82 meters for moving targets in BLE-based systems.
The integration of PIR sensors provides binary room-level presence detection with high temporal responsiveness, while pressure sensors embedded in furniture offer definitive confirmation of specific posture states such as sitting on a sofa or lying in bed. By combining these complementary data sources through a hierarchical fusion strategy, the system can overcome the inherent limitations of any single sensing modality and provide the contextual awareness necessary for reliable abnormal behavior detection in elderly home service applications.

 Anomaly Detection for Elderly Behavior Monitoring
Anomaly detection is an important aspect of elderly monitoring systems due to its significant role in detecting any unusual change in the activities of elderly people that may be abnormal or indicative of declining health due to age related disease or accidents which require immediate medical attention. With the 
ongoing
 ageing of the population and an 
ever increasing
 number of older people choosing to live independently, automated methods for anomaly detection 
have to
 be included in any smart 
home-based
 healthcare system. In Gu and Tang (2025), the authors provide a comprehensive survey of indoor abnormal behavior detection technologies for the elderly and, based on an extensive survey of the literature, they classify current approaches into three types: sensor-based, vision-based, and multi-modal fusion methods, and discuss the challenges and constraints of the technology for preserving the privacy of the older people, adapting to changing environments, and achieving real-time performance.

.1 Activity-Based Anomaly Detection Approaches
Activity of Daily Living (ADL) monitoring is typically the core part of an anomaly detection system for elderly care. In their paper titled Graph Based Anomaly Detection for Cognitive Decline of Elderly Patients in Smart Homes, published in 2020, Paudel and Eberle introduced a graph-based anomaly detection method to detect anomalies in elderly patient activities in smart homes using the concept of activity graphs. By applying the concept, the proposed approach could find temporal, spatial and behavioral patterns from the sequence of ADLs of elderly patients. Hence, anomalies in the 
residents鈥?/w:t></w:r><w:r w:rsidRPr="00C75FC4"><w:t xml:space="preserve"> activities associated with cognitive decline could be successfully identified. They added that the unusual activity patterns are usually exhibited long before the manifestation of any symptoms of cognitive decline and that the monitoring of residents鈥?activities in smart homes 
daily
 can aid in the early clinical diagnosis of cognitive decline.
We propose a novel approach for detecting unusual daily living behaviors of the elderly in their home environments by providing a system for real-time tracking of their daily activities. Our system utilizes Bluetooth beacons and a trilateration-based approach for indoor positioning. We model the normal daily activities of the elderly using a pair of Markov models, which contain transition probabilities between activities, occurrence probabilities and duration 
distributions. Furthermore, we develop a two-step approach for detecting unusual daily living behaviors, where entropy rate analysis is employed to identify the unusual sequences of activities, and cosine similarity is utilized to detect the unusual durations of the activities.

 Multi-Modal Sensor Fusion for Robust Detection
Multi-modal sensor fusion is considered as one of the effective methods to improve the reliability and accuracy of anomaly detection. Gu and Tang (2025) pointed out that using multi-modal sensor information such as wearable, environmental and vision sensors can provide complementary information to detect anomalies and alleviate the constraints or shortages of a single modality. Generally, early fusion method combines the sensor information at the input level, late fusion method combines the detection results of each sensor after processing them separately. Hybrid fusion method generally aims to achieve a balance between the speed and detection performance.
In Adibi et al. 
(2024
) a detailed study on sensor-enabled digital twins in smart healthcare environment is provided, showcasing the impact of merging IoT sensors, artificial intelligence, and digital twin technologies in smart healthcare systems. The proposed framework for a sensor-enabled digital twin framework provides real-time data monitoring and analysis for carrying out timely interventions in the context of elderly care. Digital twins can help in creating virtual copies of the physical environment and inhabitants which 
using
 simulation-based analysis can aid in predicting any abnormal behavior in the elderly and hence enables a proactive approach towards healthcare. Therefore, sensor-enabled digital twins represent a considerable leap in the field of elderly monitoring systems by moving from reactive to proactive approaches.

3.3.3 Rule-Based Anomaly Detection and Severity Grading Module
In this module, an anomaly detection engine is created to automatically identify abnormal health conditions and to classify alerts hierarchically, based on outcomes from analysis of health data. This engine establishes age-adjusted criteria for determining whether an abnormal physiological measurement has occurred based on four key indicators (i.e., heart rate, oxygen in the bloodstream, blood pressure, and body temperature) and uses motion/physical activity data to perform inclusive anomaly detection (e.g., a sharp increase in heart rate with no activity for some indicated period of time would qualify as high risk). Additionally, warning and critical severity levels will be assigned to detected anomalies based on how much individual measurements differ from established personal (baseline) and/or clinical risk criteria, with alert message generated containing structured information for each case (i.e., subject_id, anomaly_type, time of anomaly event, and severity of anomaly) that is sent to a subsequent dashboard module used to provide real-time display and stored in a database for long-term viewability.

 Indoor Positioning System
This paper proposes an indoor positioning subsystem composed of BLE beacon anchors, motion 
sensors
 and pressure 
sensors
 based on four-layer architecture namely sensing layer, communication layer, processing layer and output layer. In the sensing layer, the BLE beacon anchors are built on the ESP32-WROOM-32E chip, PIR motion sensor is arranged in each room and Force Sensitive Resistor (FSR) pressure sensor is arranged under the main furniture. The communication layer uses MQTT protocol and an Eclipse 
Mosquitto
 broker is deployed on the central server to connect with all sensor nodes. In the processing layer, the indoor positioning algorithm adopts RSSI smoothing based on Kalman filtering, distance calculation based on the log-distance path loss model, trilateration and multi-sensor fusion logic. In the output layer, the processed indoor positioning data is transmitted to the Digital Twin and the anomaly detection module.

Furniture Level Positioning: This system fuses sensor data using a hierarchical fusion algorithm that chooses the most probable and accurate position and orientation at any given time. This is achieved in 3 levels of fusion:
 
Pressure Sensors (level 1):
 
The pressure sensors on the objects (e.g. a bed) are definitive of the position of an object
: 
The system identifies the known position of an object when pressure 
sensors
 
in the matrix are activated. - Once activated, the system assumes that it has the position of the bed and tries to determine the state of people on top of it (lying or seated) based on duration of pressure sensor activation. PIR (level 2):
 
The location obtained by BLE trilateration is constrained to the room obtained from PIRs. If the PIR for a room is activated, the BLE trilateration position is cross checked against the periphery of the room. If the estimated position from trilateration is outside of the estimated 
room,
 it is assumed to be within that room. BLE (level 3):
 
In the absence of pressure sensor activation or PIR activation the system solely uses BLE trilateration for determining its position.

4.2 Indoor Positioning System Evaluation
4.2.1 Current Implementation Status
As of today, BLE RSSI acquisition and MQTT communication part is implemented and tested. It is confirmed that a single anchor (custom Arduino firmware running on an ESP32-WROOM-32E) is fully functional for the following items: - Connect to Wi-Fi and maintain a connection with 
Mosquitto
 MQTT broker; - Continuously scan and detect 
RFstar
 iBeacon in the tag through BLE; - Process the RSSI data through Kalman filter in real time; - Publish filtered data to MQTT broker at an interval of about one second. The end-to-end functionality of the whole data path from ESP32 hardware to MQTT broker and then to the client application has been validated.
The Kalman filter is parameterized with process noise covariance Q=0.5 and measurement noise covariance R=8.0, based on the few iterations of manual adjustments that were needed to achieve the desired tradeoff between tracking the small position changes as well as promptly dealing with multipath phenomena. The performance of the implemented filter is correct, in the sense that, over the considered number of iterations, the signal variance is reduced by about 22.5% at the output of the filter, while the mean RSSI is kept within 0.1 dBm from the original value, with no bias introduced. Additionally, the anchor node firmware features a reconnection routine for both Wi-Fi and MQTT links, to automatically recover from occasional loss of connection.
Our MQTT topic structure is designed with scalability and modularity in mind. Specifically, each anchor node publishes the RSSI readings to a distinct topic that includes the node ID, 
in order to
 leverage the broker鈥檚 capability for handling large number of anchor nodes in multi-anchor positioning scenarios. In addition, a status topic that periodically publishes heartbeats with a 1-minute frequency is published 
in order to
 enable the monitoring server to detect anchor node disconnection based on more than just absence of RSSI readings. The MQTT topics utilized in the positioning sub-system are summarized in Table 3.3.
